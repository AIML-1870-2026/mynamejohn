⚠ Understanding FAERS Data

The FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) is a voluntary reporting database. Before drawing conclusions from event counts, understand its limitations:

  • Voluntary reporting — not all adverse events are reported. Estimates suggest fewer than 10% of events are captured.
  • No causality — a report only means the event occurred while taking the drug. It does not prove the drug caused it.
  • Duplicate entries — the same event may be reported by the patient, doctor, and manufacturer separately.
  • Popularity bias — widely prescribed or recently publicized drugs receive more reports regardless of actual safety.
  • Missing context — reports often lack dosage, duration, and other medical history that affect interpretation.

FAERS data is best used for hypothesis generation and signal detection, not risk comparison between drugs.

FDA Recall Classifications

The FDA assigns one of three classes to drug recalls based on the health risk posed by the product:

  • Class I — Dangerous or defective products that could cause serious health consequences or death. The most serious type of recall.
  • Class II — Products that may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or where serious effects are remote.
  • Class III — Products unlikely to cause adverse health consequences but violate FDA labeling or manufacturing regulations.

Recalls can be voluntary (initiated by the manufacturer) or FDA-requested. A product being recalled does not necessarily mean it causes immediate harm.
