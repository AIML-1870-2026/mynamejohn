About This Tool

The Drug Safety Explorer lets you compare safety profiles of two drugs side-by-side using live data from the OpenFDA API. It queries three endpoints:

  • Drug Labels — official FDA-approved prescribing information including warnings, interactions, and contraindications.
  • Adverse Events (FAERS) — real-world reports of side effects submitted by patients, healthcare providers, and manufacturers.
  • Enforcement / Recalls — instances where drug products were recalled from the market.

How to Read This Data

Adverse event counts do not mean a drug is dangerous. Popular drugs that are taken by millions of people will naturally accumulate more reports than niche drugs, regardless of their actual safety profile. A high count may just mean the drug is widely prescribed.

Correlation is not causation. A FAERS report says "this patient took this drug and experienced this event." It does not prove the drug caused the event. The patient may have been taking other medications, had underlying conditions, or the event may have been coincidental.

Recall severity matters. Class I recalls involve situations where there is a reasonable probability that use of the product will cause serious adverse health consequences or death. Class II may cause temporary or medically reversible health consequences. Class III is unlikely to cause adverse health consequences.

Label data is authoritative. Drug labels are reviewed by the FDA as part of the drug approval process. They represent the most carefully vetted safety information available.

What Drug Labels Tell You

Drug label data comes from the FDA-approved Structured Product Labeling (SPL). This is the official prescribing information that accompanies every approved drug and is the most authoritative source of safety information.

Labels include warnings, contraindications, known drug interactions, and adverse reaction profiles that were identified during clinical trials. This information is reviewed by the FDA before the drug is approved and is updated over time as new safety information emerges.

Understanding Adverse Events

Adverse event data comes from the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS). These are voluntarily submitted reports — they are not the result of controlled studies.

Key things to keep in mind:

  • Higher report counts do not necessarily mean a drug is less safe. Widely prescribed drugs receive more reports simply because more people take them.
  • A report does not prove the drug caused the event. Patients often take multiple medications and have underlying conditions.
  • Reporting rates vary by drug, country, and time period. Some events are dramatically under-reported.

Despite these limitations, FAERS data has been instrumental in identifying post-market safety signals that were not detected in clinical trials.

Understanding Recall Classifications

The FDA classifies drug recalls by severity:

  • Class I — Dangerous or defective products that could cause serious health problems or death. Examples: contamination with harmful bacteria, incorrect active ingredient.
  • Class II — Products that might cause a temporary health problem or pose a slight threat of a serious nature. Examples: incorrect labeling, missing expiration dates.
  • Class III — Products that are unlikely to cause any adverse health reaction but violate FDA regulations. Examples: minor labeling errors, packaging defects.

The vast majority of drug recalls are firm-initiated (voluntary), not mandated by the FDA.

Known Dangerous Drug Pairs

Some of the most clinically significant drug interactions include:

  • Warfarin + NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Aspirin) — dramatically increases bleeding risk. NSAIDs inhibit platelet function and can cause GI ulcers, compounding warfarin's anticoagulant effect.
  • MAO Inhibitors + Serotonergic Drugs (SSRIs, tramadol) — can cause serotonin syndrome, a potentially fatal condition involving agitation, hyperthermia, and muscle rigidity.
  • Methotrexate + NSAIDs — NSAIDs reduce kidney excretion of methotrexate, leading to toxic accumulation that can damage bone marrow, liver, and kidneys.
  • ACE Inhibitors + Potassium-Sparing Diuretics — both raise potassium levels, risking hyperkalemia which can cause fatal heart arrhythmias.
